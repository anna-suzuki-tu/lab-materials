LAB OS — CHAPTER 02

Research as Design

Research stops not because of lack of ability, but because of lack of order and structure

Research stalls not from lack of data, but from lack of direction

Data increases, analysis proceeds — but what you want to show stays vague. No question. This is the most common form of research stagnation, and it has nothing to do with ability.

The failure pattern has a name: Data-First.

  1. Move your hands first — just experiment
  2. Analyze whatever came out — just analyze
  3. Exploration without direction — the wandering loop: "Meaning later"

This is not design. This is wandering.

The correct order: Framing is the blueprint

Order is everything. The right sequence is:

  1. Idea — where it starts
  2. Framing ★ — the KEY STEP — everything downstream depends on it
  3. Figures — design the figures
  4. Draft — write

Framing is the blueprint of your research. Entering the forest without a map ends today.

What is Framing? Only three things to write

  1. Core Question in one sentence
  2. State the difference: "Compared to X, we Y."
  3. Key contributions — three at most

Don't write results. Don't write methods. Just these three.

Weak framing = total collapse

✕ Vague question○ Structured question
"We studied X and found something interesting..."
What's new? Under what conditions? Unclear.		 "Compared to prior method X, our approach Y achieves Z under condition C."
Difference, result, and condition — all in one sentence

State your question in the "Compared to X, we Y" form.

Question Archetypes

Every good research question fits one of these types. A question that cannot choose an archetype is vague.

ArchetypeForm of the question
EstimationHow much / many?
MechanismHow / why does it work?
ValidityDoes method X work?
UncertaintyWhat are the limits?
ScaleDoes it scale?
DynamicsHow does it change?
DesignHow should we build X?

Once the question is fixed, the required data is decided

Question determines everything — data follows structure.

  1. Question — fixed first; structure calls in the data
  2. Required Data — back-calculated from the question
  3. Method / Experiment — methods and experiments follow; data comes last

✕ Data → hope for meaning ／ ○ Question → required data

1 figure = 1 claim

BAD ✕GOOD ○
Claim A and Claim B crammed into one figure — readers can't tell what to take away One claim per figure — Claim B goes to the next figure

Read papers via figures first. Figures are arguments, not illustrations.

Structure defines productivity — not ability

High StructureLow Structure
Framing → Figures → Draft → Submit
Design quality determines output		 Data → ??? → Repeat
Same hours, no output

Same time investment, different output — this is why we need an OS.

Paper Card: the device that fixes structure

From uncertain idea (Doubt) to confident design (Belief). The Paper Card holds five fields:

  1. Core Question — one sentence
  2. Compared to X, we Y — state the difference from prior work
  3. Key Contributions — three at most
  4. Main Figures (sketch) — layout of the figures
  5. Contribution Statement — summary of the contribution

Structure before data. No Card → No Paper.
